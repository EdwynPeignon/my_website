Spread your students

You're a teacher, you want to spread your classroom in multiple groups with the same average. Here is a tool for you. This genetic algorithm will find you a great solution.

The mark should be a decimal and the name syntax shouldn't contain any special characters.

Here are some example of syntax for the name :

  • student_*
  • student-*
  • Name Lastname

Be aware that the algorithm can take some times( at least 40 persons ). To avoid this situation you can decrease the number of generation. You can stop from the 130th generation, you'll steal have great result.

Keywords :

  • Specimen : it represent an object with specific attributes. In our case it is represented by a list of students with specific attribution to a group for each student.
  • Population : it represent the number of specimens.
  • Generation : it represent the number of reproduction of the population. The first parents will be the generation 0, their child will represent the generation 1 and so on.
  • Selection : it represent how you'll define the elite of the population.
  • Reproduction : from the selected population you'll make next generation based on them.

This genetic algorithm has a started population of 256, it's a power of 2 because I choose to make a selection by tournament. As it said 2 specimens taken randomly will be in competition, only one will be choose according to predefined criteria. They are more than one type of selection, I tried an elitist one like just keep the first half of the population. Although the problem was the population tend to look alike to each others. Selection by tournament show more diversity and has better results in the long run.

After selecting the half of the population I reproduce them to reach the initial population number. I used two types of reproduction one is to mutate a part of the elite population giving birth to a new generation with small variation. The second one was to mixed two specimen into one, you can imagine an ADN and your taking the first part of one to combine to the second ADN. And you repeat the operation till you get a satisfactory result. You maybe won't find the best solution but you'll get a great solution.

Checking the performance with Kibana

Having goods result on one try wasn't enough to ensure the performance of my algorithm. Then I indexed 100 tries results on ElasticSearch and made some visualisation on Kibana. The evaluation of a specimen was comparing the gap between the highest group average with the lowest group average. Better was the evaluation lowest was the gap. You can see below a graph showing the gap average according to ascending generation. After the 100th generation the gap in average is under 0.1.
